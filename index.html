Rationale

The problem, the knowledge gap, and why this question is interesting.

KNOWLEDGE GAP

Previous research shows that only a few animals (such as primates and certain birds) demonstrate flexible tool use, while humans can solve novel tool problems as early as age 4.

There is also research on AI, which shows key limitations: AI often requires large amounts of training data, relies on demonstrations, or depends on highly precise physics knowledge (e.g., force, motion, causality).

The main research gap is that no current AI matches human-like flexible reasoning in physical problem solving.

BIG PICTRUE QUESTION

How do humans solve new physical problems so quickly and flexibly using tools?

Why can people look at a completely new situation (like using a random object as a tool) and figure out a solution in just a few tries?

INSIGHT BOX

This study addresses a key gap between human and artificial intelligence: humans can adapt to new physical problems almost instantly, while AI cannot. Explaining this difference is crucial for understanding human cognition and improving intelligent systems.

HYPOTHESIS

Fig. B Allen et. al Sampling, simulation, and updating across mind and world

The hypothesis is that humans rely on internal knowledge of physics, combined with structured trial-and-error, rather than random guessing. This includes mental simulation, prior knowledge, and learning from failed attempts.

INSIGHT BOX

This research question breaks down a complex ability—human problem solving—into specific processes that can be tested. Instead of just asking what humans do, it asks how they do it, making it possible to model and compare human thinking with AI systems.

Fig. 2 Game levels overview

Play the Virtual Tools game yourself

There are 20 levels. In each one, you pick a tool to move the red object into the green goal. Black objects don’t move, blue ones do, and you can’t place tools in gray areas. A/B levels are paired versions.

Twenty levels were used in the Virtual Tools game. Players choose one of three tools (shown to the right of each level) to place in the scene to get a red object into the green goal area. Black objects are fixed, while blue objects also move; gray regions are prohibited for tool placement. Levels denoted with A/B labels are matched pairs.

What you're seeing

  • Levels leverage different physical concepts, such as blocking, tipping or supporting objects in a scene
  • 12 levels are matched pairs (A/B) that contain subtle differences which have large effects on the strategies needed to succeed

Constraints

  • Only one action per attempt (forces planning before acting)
  • Multiple attempts allowed (captures real-life learning: try, fail, try again)
  • Actions cannot be changed mid-trial
  • Time limit: 2 minutes per level

Each participant solved 14 levels (8 unique + 6 from matched pairs).

Variables
    Independent Variable: what you manipulated.
  • Type of level/problem (e.g., bridging, blocking, supporting)
  • Subtle differences between similar levels (matched pairs)
  • Available tools and environment setup
    Dependent Variable: what you measured.
  • Success rate
  • Number of attempts
  • Type of actions chosen
  • Learning patterns over time
  • Strategy changes
Participants
  • 94 adult participants
  • Later validation: 50 new participants
Model (SSUP)

3 STEPS

  • Sample: People choose a reasonable action to try (e.g., which tool to use and where to place it).
  • Simulate: Predicting the outcome using mental “intuitive physics”
  • Update: Improving future actions based on success or failure
This process helps explain how people can efficiently solve new problems with minimal trial

CONCEPT

Sample -> Simulate -> Update.
